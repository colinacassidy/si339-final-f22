Career

Introduction

The software engineering field has seen explosive growth in recent years, and is one of the best career paths that requires only a bachelor's degree (or even no degree). With top companies providing comfortable work life balance, competitive salaries, and many opportunities for career growth, a career in software engineering is very appealing. However, it can be a challenging industry to get into with a high barrier of entry, and a long and complex recruiting process. In this guide I hope to give a step by step breakdown of how to crack into the industry in the simplest way possible even if you aren’t a coding prodigy.

Step 1: Study

Learning to code is obviously essential for any career in software engineering or related tech fields. You can teach yourself most of what you need to know to work in the field, but if you’re studying computer science there are many classes that will help you in practical coding knowledge. An intro programming class and EECS 280 will teach you most of what you need to know about programming fundamentals, most other classes are simply an application of this knowledge.


I would recommend taking 280 then 281 as soon as possible, as these are the most beneficial classes for programming understanding and interviewing. After this, classes like EECS 485 (web systems), EECS 483/SI339 (UI/UX), or EECS 482 (operating systems) will teach you other concepts like full stack development, different programming languages and libraries, and concepts like multithreading. See my page on courses for more information on classes I’d recommend.

Step 2: Build personal projects

In addition to classes, personal projects are a great opportunity to demonstrate your skills, especially if you do not have prior internship experience. While some classes have good projects to add to your resume, many employers like to see your initiative and creativity to make your own projects, so taking project based classes as well as creating your own projects is an approach I’d recommend.


A good starting point is finding project tutorials that you can follow along with or that provide information on ideas and libraries to use. After you complete these, move on to complete your own ideas using the knowledge you learned from the walkthrough. This is an effective way to both learn and create projects for your resume.

Step 3: Write a resume

Once you have learned computer science fundamentals and put them to use in classes and personal projects, the next step is to put it all together in a resume to show potential employers your skills in a concise document. I like this template from reddit , which is similar to what I use on my own resume. Generally simple formatting is best, as it will perform better in auto resume screening software that is frequently used.


I would recommend putting education at the top of the resume, and keep it fairly short with details on major, graduation date, relevant classes taken, and GPA if it's above a 3.0.


Next I would put relevant experience next if you have any. This could be previous internships or relevant research or IA work. I would put more detail into this section, as past experience is a major factor recruiters look for. Using action words and numbers to describe and quantify impact will make this section stand out.


Regardless of if you have previous experience, the projects section allows you to showcase your ability to put your skills to use, as well as your initiative to develop your skills in your own time. If you don’t have any previous experience, this section can be great to demonstrate your ability to work on large complex projects in absence of actual work experience.


Finally, sections for skills and extracurriculars/leadership are good to round out your resume. List any programming languages or common frameworks/libraries you are familiar with. An extracurricular/leadership section shows your ability to work well in groups, regardless of if the activity is related to computer science or not.

Step 4: Prepare to interview

Many top companies give coding interviews that require strong understanding of computer science fundamentals and data structures and algorithms. EECS 281 will teach you everything you need to know for these interviews, so I would recommend taking it as soon as possible. Regardless of if you have taken EECS 281, practicing for these types of interviews is essential to getting into top software engineering companies.


Leetcode has many commonly asked coding interview questions, and lists like blind75 or leetcode patterns provide common questions in every major category of coding question. Books like Elements of Coding Interviews and Cracking the Coding Interview also provide comprehensive guides to common types of questions and how to solve them.

Step 5: Apply to jobs

Most jobs for SWE internships and new grad jobs open in late July to August, altho many come out in following months. One of the easiest ways to increase your chances of success is to apply early. Not only will you get a decision faster, but interviews tend to be easier earlier in the season, and you don’t have to worry about positions filling up.


Early career jobs in software engineering is largely a numbers game, so the best strategy is to mass apply to all types of companies, from FAANG+ to local tech companies. I’ve found for first internships, smaller local tech companies as well as healthcare companies like UHG and Cigna tend to have higher success rates. Companies like Amazon and Microsoft are among the easier FAANG+ companies, so I would highly recommend applying to these especially. Either way, applying to 100+ companies greatly increases your chances of success. Lists like this github page one have links to hundreds of available internships.

Step 6: Interview tips

Interviewing at tech companies tends to follow this general structure. First, an online coding assessment that can be completed at any time. These are timed, and are assessed on completion and number of test cases passed, so I would recommend using a language like python that is quick to code with if you are comfortable with it.


Next is the main screening, the technical interview. Depending on the company and position, there can be anywhere from 1 to 5 rounds of these, although for internships, it tends to be 1-2 rounds, with one pure technical, and one final interview with either behavioral elements or system design.


Use what you’ve learned in interview prep, and walk through your solution step by step. Being able to explain your logic and communicate effectively is just as important as your ability to solve the problem. I tend to walk through an obvious solution with poor time or space complexity to stall for time, then work towards the more efficient solution they are looking for. This gives me more time to think and come up with an effective solution.

Step 7: Getting offers

Assuming everything has gone well, you should end the recruiting season with at least one offer. If you only have one offer, the decision is easy, otherwise there are several factors to consider when choosing somewhere to work. While pay is appealing, the most important factors to consider is the ability to learn relevant skills, and the opportunities provided by the company. Early in one’s career, gaining experience and learning is one one of the best investments to make. Clout of the company is also something to consider, as having that company on your resume could open doors to top companies. Regardless of what you pick, you are well on your way to beginning your career with an offer secured.

Next steps

Now that you have received and accepted your offer, the next step is to start the Job. If you are working an internship, you have the opportunity to learn a lot about the software engineering field and what it’s like to work for that company. In addition, an internship serves as a 12 week long interview for the company to determine if you would be a good fit as a full time employee. Making sure you meet your goals ensures that you get the most out of your experience and maximize your chances of getting a return offer for another internship or a full time position.
